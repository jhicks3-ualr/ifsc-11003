Thoughts on Progressive Enhancement

In my understanding of Progressive Enhancement, rather than create some that is only fully functional for some and passable for others, you instead create something that is fully functional for all. The key to this design it seems, is working from your content outwards. You focus on rich well organized content first in your HTML, making sure the structures make sense, that accessibility labels are in place, that their is a uniform system, content is the priority. Then your art begins with the CSS, create your design add your colors, ensure your content does can be understood and accessed by all, style and browser features are secondary to content. Then finally the JS to add functions and go from a 2D picture to a pop up book. At each step the End User of the lowest level functionality is the one being considered first, and then layers are added on once those needs are met. Final Project Revision and Reflection

For my final project, once I settled on which content I wanted, I asked for feedback at multiple stages. My Wife helped me pickout my background image, it was her idea to go with the spooky house rather than the shifting color background I had originally decided on. My buddy Luis encouraged me to do the dark/light theme and we designed that together. I also corrected certain font sizes, and moved certain elements and colors (such as the table hover highlight effect) based on their feedback. Again alot of it was throughout the whole process not just from first draft to final. I did maintain certain settings we had gone over in class, such as the page links in the header because I liked the look and it just made sense with what I was going with. I think the only recommendation that I didn't go with was Luis said it'd be fun to have a video of trailers from some of the movies involving the two characters. I vetoed that idea because I'm somewhat of a purist in regards to the literature, and also I didn't feel it fit well with the content.

My biggest learning challenge was CSS specificity, flex and grids. If I was starting over I would have better class/id categorization from the start, instead of having to go back and correct things in the html. Using flex was useful, but it did not come without its breaking points, but I did find it better for move small elements around when you need everything else stationary. Grid was my favorite tool though, every page contains grids, and sometimes grids within grids, I did have a bit of a learning curve but once I got the hang of it, grids became a routine function on the page.

I decided to add the .slick function to my final project because during class I had the idea to showcase some of my favorite books and thought that was a perfect way to do so, and I liked getting to use more javascript. I didnt change much other than the colors and the cssEase and autoplay speed because I still wanted it to make sense for the content, but I did play around with the different settings. I really enjoyed this class, I like getting to make art with technology, also getting to show off all that progress and work is really fun. I think my favorite aspect of this class was the CSS, learning how to make my web pages look artistic and stylized, or rudimentary and optimal, just based on a few lines of code and alittle imagination is a really fun concept. I'm hoping to take on a web design project for a local non-profit soon just as a way to keep practicing and add to my portfolio.
