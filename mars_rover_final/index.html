The Plot Thickens

The objective of this blog is information. In reading through the stories of Sherlock Holmes and Hercule Poirot, it's difficult not to compare the two literary detectives. Especially in Agatha Christie's "Murder on the Links", which is the second book in the Hercule Poirot series, where she makes very direct comparisons between Holmes and Poirot, and even introduces a new character by the name of Giraud who is a distinctly Holmes like character, and ends up as Poirot's rival in the novel. So who is the better detective? Unfortunately, that is up to each individual reader. Do I have my own opinion? Yes. Will I voice my opinion? No. My only intent is to provide information and let YOU the reader make your own decision.

Fun and Random Facts

  • Although he created the most famous logician in history, Arthur Conan Doyle was a staunch believe in magic and mysticism, and was even close friends with Harry Houdini.
  • After meeting Oscar Wilde at the Langham Hotel in 1889, many Sherlockians (or Holmesians in the UK) believe Conan Doyle altered Sherlock Holmes' personality and habits to mirror that of Oscar Wilde, whom Doyle was a huge fan of.
  • Arthur Conan Doyle was knighted in 1902 for his non-fiction writing in which he defended Britains actions in the Boer War.
  • The initial character for Sherlock Holmes was inspired by a professor of Doyles in medical school, who was famous for diagnosing patients without touching them.
  • Argueablly the most famous line of Sherlock Holmes, "Elementary, My Dear Watson" is never actually said by Holmes in the original stories.
  • According to IMDB, Sherlock Holmes is the most potrayed human character on film and television.
  • Holmes was very rarely described as wearing his famed deerstalker cap in the books, typically only for country cases, the cap and tweed jacket were made famous by illustrator Sidney Paget
  • Agatha Christie is the best-selling novelist in history with around 2 billion copies sold, ranking only behind Shakespeare and the Bible.
  • Christie was the first British woman to surf standing up.
  • Christie worked as an apothecary's assistant during WWI, which gave her an exstensive knowledge of poisions that she used in her books.
  • Christie once disappeared for 11 days, sparking a manhunt, only to be found at a spa under a false name (a true legend).
  • Poirot's mustache was meant to be "the most magnificent", and Christie once objected to a film potrayal for not doing the mustache justice.
  • Her book Murder in Mesopotamia was inspired by archaeological digs she went on with her husband.
  • Poirot was the first and only fictional character to have his obituary published on the front page of the New York Times.
